Why?

Annually, the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (UNOCHA) releases the Global Humanitarian Overview, which details enduring humanitarian crises. We saw it. We wanted to learn more. So, we built a tool. And now we want to share that tool with you.

What?

Our algorithm analyzes winners of the Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting and captures a similarity score for any article that we feed it. Curently, we use it to suggest powerful articles about pressing humanitarian crises.

Frequently Asked Questions

What next?
We are currently working on improving the algorithm's capacity to make powerful suggestions and ignore extraneous, unrelated articles. But we are altogether convinced that the algorithm is just a start: there is a mob of unanswered questions waiting to be addressed and an algorithm can only do so much to answer them. To that end, we cannot wait to grow, improve and see where our ideas take us.
How does the algorithim work? What metrics do you use?
Our algorithim uses a powerful neural network library written in Python: Keras. We compute lingual and sentiment data (we use TextBlob for the latter, a natural language processing toolkit) for Pulitzer-winning and non-Pulitzer-winning articles alike. We train a model, which, combined with our ranking algorithim, allows us to produce sets of ranked articles. These articles are stored in our database daily, and high-ranking articles are presented on the website for you to read.

It is not perfect and we are always looking for ways to improve it, but it does generate powerful suggestions and we are beyond excited to share it with you in this capacity.
